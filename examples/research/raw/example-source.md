# Example source — The shape of scientific argument

Scientific argument typically proceeds in four moves: framing a question, staking
a claim, marshalling evidence, and acknowledging limits. Each move can be
examined as a rhetorical act and as an epistemic one; the two are not separable.

When a researcher writes "X causes Y," they commit to at least three things:
a definition of X, a definition of Y, and a theory of how one could falsify
the causal link. The first two are often left implicit, which is why so much
argument in practice is about definitions rather than data.

The strongest papers are the ones where the limits section does real work:
where the authors name what their evidence cannot support, and where future
work could sharpen the claim.

## Key ideas
- Scientific writing is simultaneously rhetorical and epistemic.
- Definitional disputes underlie many empirical disagreements.
- A genuine limits section strengthens, rather than weakens, a claim.
